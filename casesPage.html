Reynolds v. United States

Decided on January 6, 1879

Can Congress legislate a practice such as polygamy even if a religion views it as their right?

Ruled 9-0 in favor of United States

Yes. Religion cannot be used as a defense for committing crime; further, Congress can legislate certain practices even if a religion argues it’s part of their faith if the practice is not in the common interest. Brought up that allowing polygamy would set precedent which could allow human sacrifice, etc.

×

Cantwell v. Connecticut

Decided on May 20, 1940

Can a religious person partake in door-to-door evangelization?

Ruled 9-0 in favor of Cantwell

Yes. Protects evangelization – door to door ministering – under the 1st Amendment.

×

Minersville School District v. Gobitis

Decided on June 3, 1940

Can a school compel students to say the pledge if it goes against their religion?

Ruled 8-1 in favor of Minersville School District

Yes. Schools can compel students to say the pledge regardless of their religion.

Sudden Reversal

Just three years later, faced with a case nearly identical to Minersville, the Court changes tune:

×

West Virginia State BOE v. Barnette

Decided on June 14, 1943

Can a school compel students to say the pledge if it goes against their religion?

Ruled 6-3 in favor of Barnette

No. Overruling Minersville v Gobitis, the Court declares that pledge compulsions violate the First Amendment.

×

Engel v. Vitale

Decided on June 25, 1962

Can a public-school hold prayer in the morning?

Ruled 6-1 in favor of Engel

No. School prayer, even non-compulsory, in public schools is a violation of the establishment clause and thus unconstitutional.

Two Justices did not participate in this case.

×

Sherbert v. Verner

Decided on June 17, 1963

Can a state deny unemployment benefits to someone who was fired for refusing to work on a day which their religion compels them not to?

Ruled 7-2 in favor of Shert

No. Being fired for refusing to work because of religious reasons – the Sabbath, in this case – does not prevent someone from receiving benefits.

×

Flast v. Cohen

Decided on June 10, 1968

Can a taxpayer sue the state for using tax funds to purchase textbooks for a religious school?

Ruled 8-1 in favor of Flast

Yes. If a taxpayer can prove that there is a link between them and the legislation, and that the legislation exceeds Constitutional limits, then they have standing to sue.

×

Epperson v. Arkansas

Decided on November 12, 1968

Can state law forbid teaching something because it goes against the religious beliefs of certain groups of people?

Ruled 9-0 in favor of Epperson

No. States cannot prohibit teaching something because it goes against the religious beliefs of certain members of the state.

×

Lemon v. Kurtzman

Decided on June 28, 1971

Can a state provide for the salary of non-secular teachers?

Ruled 8-1 in favor of Lemon

No. The law in question resulted in excessive government entanglement with religion. Established the three-pronged test • Statute must have a secular legislative purpose • Primary purpose of statute must neither advance nor inhibit religion • Statute must not result in excessive government entanglement with religion.

Controvery is born

The Lemon Test, once established, marked the start of a series of controversial decisions. Conservative Justices Clarence Thomas and Antonin Scalia have criticized the three-pronged test.

×

Stone v. Graham

Decided on November 17, 1980

Can a state require a religious text to be displayed in public schools?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Stone

No. By the three-pronged Lemon Test, a state cannot require posting religious texts in public school classrooms without violating the Establishment Clause.

×

Mueller v. Allen

Decided on June 29, 1983

Can a state allow taxpayers to deduct expenses incurred from spending on tuition, books, etc., for a religious school?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Allen

Yes. By the three-pronged Lemon test, the law does not explicitly advance religious purposes; rather, it simply applies tax deductions evenly rather than singling out schools because of their religion.

×

Bob Jones University v United States & Goldsboro Christian Schools Inc. v United States

Decided on May 24, 1983

Can the government prohibit funding to schools engaged in racial discrimination if said discrimination is a religious belief?

Ruled 8-1 in favor of United States

Yes. The government may limit religious freedom if it is required to achieve an “overriding governmental interest.” Both cases were decided in the same case.

×

Lynch v. Donnelly

Decided on March 5, 1984

Are public displays with a religious history a violation of the establishment clause?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Lynch

Yes. Not necessarily. In the case of the Nativity scene, while it has a history in religion, in context of the holidays, it was not inherently religious and had “legitimate secular purposes.” Religious symbols which are commonly associated with a holiday more generally are not a violation of the establishment clause. Applied the Lemon Test.

×

Employment Division v. Smith

Decided on April 17, 1990

Can a state deny benefits to someone who breaks the law with religious justification?

Ruled 6-3 in favor of Employment Division

No. A neutral law generally applied does not infringe on religious freedom just because someone’s religious beliefs are contradictory

×

Church of the Lukumi Babula Aye, Inc. v City of Hialeah

Decided on June 11, 1993

In prohibiting animal sacrifice, did the city of Hialeah violate the First Amendment?

Ruled 9-0 in favor of Church of Lukumi

Yes. The ordinance as passed specifically targeted the Santeria faith and was not applied neutrally or was generally applicable. Used Employment Division v. Smith as precedent.

×

City of Boerne v. Flores

Decided on April 17, 1990

Is the RFRA an unconstitutional use of Congress’ enforcement power when it comes to the acts enforcement on state and local ordinances?

Ruled 6-3 in favor of City of Boerne

Yes. Congress cannot define the rights guaranteed by the 14th Amendment – only the Courts can. Portions of the RFRA as applied to state and local ordinances exceed the enforcement powers that Congress has.

×

Locke v. Davey

Decided on February 25, 2004

Can a state deny scholarship funds to a degree pursuing a religious end?

Ruled 7-2 in favor of Locke

Yes. States can choose to decline to fund through scholarships religious programs.

×

Hein v. Freedom of Religion

Decided on June 25, 2007

Do taxpayers have the standing to sue a federally funded religious outreach program because of its religious nature?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Locke

No. Taxpayers do not have a standing to sue the government for funding a religiously associated government entity just because of its’ religious nature. Does not extend the Flast v Cohen exception.

×

Christian Legal Society v. Martinez

Decided on June 28, 2010

Can a university prevent a student group from accessing funds if the groups religion requires them to discriminate and violate school nondiscrimination policy?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Martinez

Yes. Nondiscrimination policies can remain in place in universities and prevent groups on campuses from discriminating against students because of their sexual orientation. Freedom of speech does not necessarily mean you can discriminate. Helps cement sexual orientation as a status, not a belief.

×

Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church and School v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission

Decided on January 11, 2012

Does the ministerial exception apply to teachers at a religious school?

Ruled 9-0 in favor of Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church and School

Yes. Because Hosanna-Tabor held Perich - the teacher in question - as a minister, the ministerial exception applied.

×

Hobby Lobby v. Burwell

Decided on June 30, 2014

Can a company be compelled to provide certain healthcare services which it believes is against it’s religious belief?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Hobby Lobby

No. A company can deny certain types of covergae if it believes itself to be religious and that type of coverage is against its' religion. Allows certain types of corporations to claim religious belief, something which previously only individuals could do.

×

Espinoza v. Montana Department of Revenue

Decided on June 30, 2020

Can a state explicitly block tax deductable donations to scholarship programs from being used at religious schools because of their religion?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Espinoza

No. States cannot single out schools from receiving these types of funds explicitly because of their religion. Prevents states from blocking voucher programs from being used at religious schools because of their religious identity, bringing into question the constitutionality of Blaine Amendments.

Case rules in a way almost entirely different from earlier similar case, Locke v David. However, similar reasoning is used in this case as in Mueller v Allen.

The Divide Deepens

As the Court has, over the years, become more dictated by partisan ideology, cases regarding controversial topics of religion have increasingly been ruled along party lines. This sharp divide is particularly evident during the COVID-19 pandemic, where the addition of conservative justice Amy Coney Barret flipped the decision of nearly identical cases regarding restrictions on gathering sizes of religious groups. Increasingly, 5-4 rulings are the norm rather than an exception.

South Bay United Pentacostal Church v. Gavin Newsom

Decided on May 29, 2020

Can a state place stricter gathering restrictions on religious gatherings?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Gavin Newsom

Yes. So long as the restrictions are not stricter than similar secular gatherings, a state can strictly limit gathering sizes of a religion in the face of a pandemic.

Sudden Reversal

Months and a Justice nomination later, faced with an identical case, the Court changes tune:

×

Roman Catholic Diocese of Brooklyn, New York v Andrew M Cuomo

Decided on November 25, 2020

Can a state place stricter gathering restrictions on religious gatherings?

Ruled 5-4 in favor of Barnette

No. In a reversal to earlier similar decisions – because of the changed makeup of the Court – the Court ruled that religious gatherings cannot be more strictly restricted than less similar secular gatherings.

Conclusions

What can it all mean?

Throughout history, the Supreme Court often errs on the side of caution – in nearly all cases examined, freedoms of religion have been increased. However, this is not always applied equally. Often, as seen with the COVID-19 gathering restriction cases, how the Court rules can change based on the makeup of the Court itself, rather than a change in the law or time. Freedom of Religion is perhaps then, rather than something rigid, a fluid thing, changing with the times.
